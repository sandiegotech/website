IIdentity

The Founding Charter Aesthetic

SDIT's visual language is that of an institution that has existed for a long time and knows it will exist for a long time more. Every design decision should reinforce that sense: restraint over decoration, permanence over trend, character over cleverness.

The reference point is not a startup, a university marketing site, or a Silicon Valley product. It is a founding document — the kind printed on rag paper and signed with care. The look is a consequence of that intention.

On dark backgrounds, use warm white at 90% opacity for primary text.

Secondary text at 62% opacity. Metadata at 40%. Never pure white — always #FCFBF8 derived.

VIVoice & Tone

How SDIT writes

The voice is that of an institution that has thought carefully and speaks plainly. No marketing puffery. No hedging. No casual register. Every sentence earns its place.

Register Formal but not stiff. The register of a well-written letter, not a legal brief and not a newsletter.
Sentences Short-to-medium. Declarative. Vary cadence: short statement, then expansion. Avoid passive voice.
First person "We" (institutional), never "I" in copy. Use second person "you" sparingly and only in direct address (application, donation pages).
Avoid "Innovative," "passionate," "world-class," "doing good," "change-maker," "community." These undercut seriousness.
Dates Roman numerals for years in display contexts: MMXXVI. Numeric for prose.
Em dashes Use liberally — they are the natural pause of this voice. Always surrounded by spaces.
Italics For key phrases in display text (use <em> in HTML — renders in marine blue). Avoid overuse in body copy.

Do

"Industrial education trained people to work like machines. Machines now do that work."

"We are at the beginning, deliberately."

"Every application is read personally."

Don't

"We're passionate about transforming education for the 21st century!"

"Join our innovative community of change-makers."

"Apply now and unlock your potential."

Critical framing rule

Surfing, film, photography, music, skating — these are subjects of rigorous study, not lifestyle benefits. Never present them as feature cards or "study what you love" marketing. The right framing weaves them into serious prose: "A wave is a problem in fluid dynamics and a lesson in nerve." The instant they become selling points, credibility evaporates.

VIIApplication

Applying the system

Rules for extending the brand to new surfaces — email, print, presentations, and third-party platforms.

Email White background is acceptable for email clients. Use paper (#FCFBF8) if supported. EB Garamond for subject/headline only — use system serif as fallback. IBM Plex Sans for body throughout.
Print Off-white stock preferred (100–110gsm uncoated). Navy + gold only — no screen colors. Full crest (gold version) for letterhead and cover pages. Never scale the crest below 25mm wide.
Presentations Slide background: #FCFBF8. Titles: EB Garamond 500. Body: IBM Plex Sans. Accent lines: gold hairlines. No gradients, no drop shadows, no custom shapes beyond rectangles and ruled lines.
Social Navy tile background, gold or white crest. Mono ALL CAPS label at top, Garamond quote below. No filters. No emojis in official content. Avoid social-native design patterns (rounded avatars, gradient overlays).
Third-party platforms Use the navy + gold palette wherever possible. If a platform forces its own design (e.g. PayPal button), contain it in a .give-card panel to frame it as an inset widget rather than a visual element of the page.
Photography Desaturated or naturally warm tones. No stock photography. Preference for honest documentary imagery — real people doing real work. Never posed "inspiration" photography.
What breaks the system Rounded cards, colored background panels, drop shadows, sans-serif headings, bright accent colors, gradient fills, hero imagery with text overlays, ALL-CAPS in display headings.
