SDIT Research · Gloss · Language

What does it mean to understand a word rather than look it up?

A fellowship research project into language as the instrument of thought — and what it looks like to use AI to deepen comprehension rather than bypass it.

Fellowship Research · Free · MIT · iOS
The Research QuestionLanguage

A person who skips the words they do not know learns to skim.

A person who stops and asks learns to read — and reading is where all other learning begins.

The question Gloss investigates is where the line falls between AI as substitute and AI as aid. A tool that reads for you removes the reader. A tool that removes the friction from stopping at an unknown word — which is what Gloss does — leaves the reader intact while making the reader better. This distinction matters in every domain: AI can do the work, or AI can make it easier to do the work yourself. The Institute holds that the second is the correct design for a human being in this era.

A fellowship researcher at SDIT built Gloss to test whether the oldest habit of serious readers — stopping at the word that stopped them — can be restored by reducing its cost from a broken concentration to five seconds.

The InstrumentThe app

A dictionary that has read the whole paragraph.

Definitions tell you what a word can mean. Gloss tells you what it means right here, in the sentence in front of you.

Select a word anywhere on your iPhone — in Safari, Books, Mail, or any app — and share it to Gloss. Share a whole paragraph and every word in it becomes tappable. The passage rides along as context; the explanation arrives in the voice of the text you were already reading. Ask follow-up questions to go deeper. The passage is still yours to read; Gloss removes the one obstacle most likely to cause a reader to give up.

Why It MattersThe stakes

Language is the medium of thought. To master it is to master thinking.

AI can produce language fluently. What it cannot do is give you the experience of understanding it — the moment when a word you did not know becomes one you do, and the passage opens up.

That experience is irreplaceable, and it is the foundation of every other form of understanding. The people who will command the age of AI are those who command language — who can read the outputs, evaluate the arguments, and form the words of their own judgments. The breadth of vocabulary is not an ornament; it is the size of the space in which a person can think.

The fellowship researchers of SDIT believe that tools should make this capacity more natural, not less necessary. Gloss exists because the question it investigates — what AI looks like when it deepens a mind instead of replacing it — has no answer more important than this one.
