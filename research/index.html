SDIT Research · Lectio · Fellowship Project

Let any person read a great work. With genuine ease.

Lectio is a reading app for the great works of the Western canon — Homer, Plato, Dante, Darwin, Camus — built on an AI-produced modern edition reviewed by human scholars, a comprehension layer that lives beside the text, and a model that makes every word free to read and every printed volume a means of funding this institution.

It is the primary fellowship research project of the San Diego Institute of Technology. It is a large undertaking, and it is underway.

The Research QuestionReading

The greatest books ever written are almost entirely unread.

Not because people aren't capable. Because the on-ramp is brutal — and no one has fixed it.

The Harvard Classics — 51 volumes, assembled in 1909 as the education any serious person could give themselves in fifteen minutes a day — are free, in the public domain, and contain some of the most important things ever written: The Iliad, Meditations, The Divine Comedy, Moby-Dick, On the Origin of Species, Democracy in America. And almost nobody reads them.

The reason is not laziness. It is the on-ramp: archaic translations that put even native English speakers at a remove, names and references that assume a classical education no one has anymore, dense walls of text with no context and no margin for the mind. Existing reading apps solve none of this — they hand you the same forbidding 1909 file and wish you luck.

The research question Lectio investigates is what happens when you fix that. If you give a modern reader a clear, plain-English edition of The Iliad — synthesized from the full scholarly record, with context beside the text and every hard word a tap away — does the book become readable? The hypothesis is yes. And that the right instrument for this is not a summary, not a podcast, not an audiobook: it is the book itself, properly made for the reader it has now.

This is also a question about AI. Lectio holds that AI can either replace reading or deepen it, and that the design of every feature must choose between those two. A tool that reads for you removes the reader. A tool that removes the friction from stopping at an unknown word — or that gives you a plain-English edition of Dante instead of a nineteenth-century verse translation — leaves the reader intact while making the reader better. That distinction is the research.

The Lectio Modern EditionThe flagship

A fresh, plain-modern-English edition of each work. Produced by deep research. Free in the app.

The thing only Lectio does: a new edition of each great work, synthesized from the full scholarly record and expressed in the language people actually read now.

Not a summary. Not a simplification. A real, faithful rendering of the work — the sense and force of the original, in contemporary English — produced by a rigorous process and read by a human before it ships. A person who reads the Lectio Modern Edition of The Iliad has read The Iliad.

How it is made. For each section of text, the research pipeline gathers everything available: every public-domain translation, the original Greek or Latin, and all available scholarship — modern translations as research input, academic commentaries, the ancient scholia. It synthesizes from the full record and produces a rendering that is faithful to the original, informed by every serious reading of it, and expressed in original language. Then a human reads and approves it before it ships. Copyrighted translations are research inputs, never reproduced text — what scholarship says about a passage can be synthesized; the text of a copyrighted translation cannot be included.

Provenance is a first-class feature. Every Lectio Modern Edition carries a full provenance record: the AI model used, the date produced, the sources consulted, and a plain-language description of the method. The reading surface shows only "Lectio Modern Edition." The full record is one tap away. It is never passed off as a historical translation.

Editions have vintages. As models improve, the edition can be regenerated — a 2026 edition, a 2028 edition, and so on. The current edition is always the default. Older vintages are archived with their provenance intact: a permanent, citable record. Students citing a passage can pin to a specific vintage.

The Lectio Modern Edition is the default in the app. One tap switches to any public-domain translation or the original language. Accessibility by default, depth on demand.

The CorpusThe scope

Starting with three. The scope: the entire Harvard Classics.

In 1909, Charles Eliot — outgoing president of Harvard — assembled 51 volumes he called the five-foot shelf: the works a person would need to read, in fifteen minutes a day, to give themselves a genuine education. They are still in print. They are in the public domain. They contain some of the most consequential thinking in the history of civilization.

Lectio is being built to make all of them readable — not just accessible, but readable: the kind of reading where you finish and feel the work was worth the time. The production process begins with three:

Meditations

Marcus Aurelius. Stoic philosophy written by a Roman emperor to himself. In the app now with the Modern Edition, the George Long 1862 translation, Méric Casaubon 1634, and the original Greek.

The Iliad

Homer. In the app now. The showcase for multi-translation reading: Pope's heroic couplets, Butler's plain prose, and the original Greek — all at once, all in place.

Apology · Crito · Phaedo

Plato. The trial and death of Socrates — short, gripping, and far more approachable for a first reader than anything else in the Greek philosophical canon.

Getting these three right — especially the Modern Edition quality — is the whole game for the first phase. Breadth comes after. Custom covers have been produced for over 130 works; the catalog is ready to scale as the production process is proven.

The MarginComprehension layer

Context beside the text. The reading column stays calm.

The signature UI of Lectio: a warm reading column on the left, a quiet right-hand rail — The Margin — carrying the living layer. Tap a word, see a person, follow a reference. All of it in the margin, none of it interrupting the text.

The comprehension layer is pre-generated, not live. For each work, the research pipeline produces an annotation dictionary: 300 to 400 entries covering proper nouns, recurring concepts, and the high-frequency references a modern reader will stumble on. Who is Achilles? What is xenia? What are the Furies? Each entry is generated once from the full scholarly record, human-reviewed, and committed to the content bundle as static, finished data. A reader tapping "xenia" gets a considered, edited definition — not whatever a model generates cold that afternoon.

Words not in the annotation dictionary fall through to Webster's 1913 — a complete, public-domain dictionary included in the app. No API call for ordinary vocabulary. On-device AI handles anything genuinely unexpected. The design goal is near-zero per-user cost: content is pre-generated and served static, so each additional reader costs essentially nothing.

Multi-edition reading. Every work carries multiple editions: the Lectio Modern Edition, every public-domain translation available, and the original-language text. Switching editions keeps your place. Each edition carries a provenance card — translator and year for historical editions; model, date, and sources for Lectio editions — available in the switcher and in any exported PDF.

What's built The Margin, tap-to-explore words, word save, editorial enrichment per passage, and multi-edition reading are all running. Meditations, The Iliad, The Odyssey, and The Aeneid are in the content bundle.
The Lectio Edition in PrintThe artifact

The app is free. The book is the product. The proceeds fund the university.

A beautifully made printed Lectio Edition is the natural physical form of this work — and the revenue model that makes everything else possible.

Lectio the app is free end-to-end: the public-domain texts, the original languages, The Margin, the annotation dictionary, and the Lectio Modern Edition itself. No subscription, no paywall on the text. The near-zero AI cost architecture — content pre-generated and served static — makes free sustainable at any scale.

Revenue comes from the physical artifact. A printed Lectio Edition of each work — beautifully designed, human-read and approved in full before printing, carrying a provenance colophon — is produced on demand. No inventory. No overhead. No risk. Whoever wants only to read pays nothing; whoever wants to keep a fine edition buys the book. Proceeds flow through SDIT and fund the university.

Every work and every language is a SKU with zero carrying cost. The catalog compounds. Spanish editions — fitting San Diego — come next. This is not a separate product from the app; it is another rendering of the same content, made permanent. The knowledge is never gated. Only the made object is sold.

A modern translation that tells you exactly how it was made — model, vintage, sources, human reviewer — is the selling point, not a disclaimer. Honesty is the product.
